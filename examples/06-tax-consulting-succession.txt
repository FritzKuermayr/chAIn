Three issues we're trying to resolve, all interlocking.

1) CFC / Hinzurechnungsbesteuerung exposure

With the post-2022 ATAD-implementing rules (§§7-14 AStG, current version), we have substantial uncertainty about whether the B.V.'s passive-income classification triggers CFC inclusion at the GmbH level. The local NL tax counsel (Mr. Joris Bakker at Loyens & Loeff, j.bakker@loyensloeff.com) claims the B.V.'s "low-tax" rate is above the 25% threshold and argues no CFC. Our reading is more cautious. The NL corporate tax has two-tier structure with 19% on the first EUR 200k and 25.8% above. After deducting the actually applicable innovation-box-equivalent treatment (which doesn't apply here, the B.V. has no qualifying IP), and including the relevant base allowance, we calculate the effective NL rate for the B.V. at ~24.6% in FY2025 — three-tenths of a percent below the AStG threshold.

If we're right, that's a Hinzurechnung at the German shareholder level (the GmbH) for the proportion of the B.V.'s income classified as "passive." The 90% intra-group billing pattern makes that classification arguable both ways. Our position: customs-broker services billed to a related party are still active business income because the underlying activity is operational service delivery, not passive licensing/IP/financing. NL counsel agrees. The risk is that the BfF (Bundeszentralamt für Steuern) takes a different view in a Betriebsprüfung in 2027 or 2028, and by that point we've already executed the gift transfers and unwinding the position retroactively is painful.

Maria's audit team flagged this in their preliminary memo last month (Mazars memo MZ-2026-0294, dated 2026-03-22). 2) The succession structure and the Verschonung threshold

Klaus wants to transfer 60% over four years using the favored §13a/13b ErbStG regime. The standard Verschonungsabschlag of 85% applies because the GmbH is below the EUR 26M threshold for the Großvermögen reduction. The catch: the Rotterdam B.V. is potentially Verwaltungsvermögen-relevant (it's a passive holding by some readings). And if its classification flips because of issue (1) above — i.e. if we treat the B.V. as a non-operating subsidiary for German tax purposes — we lose the Verschonung on roughly 18% of the gift base, which translates to a transfer tax of approximately EUR 720,000 that Sarah personally cannot fund without selling shares back to Klaus, defeating the whole point of the gift.

3) Operational dependency on the Rotterdam entity

We've considered three structural paths.

Path A: Restructure the B.V. before the gift — collapse the Rotterdam entity into a German GmbH subsidiary with a Dutch branch instead. Operationally this is awkward: the chemical client (Berghaus Chemie AG) requires a Dutch-resident entity for the customs-broker license (DCBA license #NL-2018-3392, issued by Douane Nederland, valid until 2028-12-31, transferable only with significant administrative process). Loss of the license = loss of EUR 7.6M of the EUR 8.4M Rotterdam revenue, which destroys the operational logic of the entity entirely.

Path B: Restructure the B.V. so the passive-income share drops below the threshold — would require redirecting the Berghaus intra-group billing through a real third-party arrangement (perhaps via an external customs broker partner contractually inserted in the chain, with a margin uplift). The TP people (currently BDO, last study BDO-TP-2024-09 from September 2024, file ref BDO-MUE-TP-09-2024) will want to model carefully and would likely require a Sicherheitsabschlag of 8-12% on the gross margin to produce a Betriebsprüfung-defensible structure. That still leaves the B.V. profitable and operational.

Path C: Accept the CFC inclusion, lose part of the §13a Verschonung, but use the EUR 720k cash from the term-loan refinance proceeds (Commerzbank approved an extended facility up to EUR 12M last week, loan ref CB-CRE-2026-MUE-0294, drawdown available 2026-06-01) to fund the resulting transfer tax liability.
